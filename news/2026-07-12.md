## Counter-narrative

The day's biggest story is Apple v. OpenAI, day two. The skeptical case:

- **Trade-secrets litigation is slow, discovery-heavy, and frequently settles quietly.** The "LOL, I found out I can access the [network storage]" quote is press-cycle catnip, but even taken at face value it evidences one engineer's individual misconduct — the harder bar Apple has to clear is systemic direction from OpenAI to procure that access. Prior high-profile tech-employee trade-secrets fights (Waymo v. Uber, Anthropic v. former exec, etc.) have generally produced settlements or narrow injunctions, not company-scale strategic damage. Buyers pausing OpenAI hardware conversations on the basis of a filing risk underweighting the base rate that this ends in a payment and a promise.
- **The "400+ ex-Apple employees at OpenAI" datapoint is unremarkable in context.** Apple has ~170,000 employees; a 0.24% cross-hire over two-plus years of aggressive OpenAI hardware hiring is normal Silicon Valley churn, not smoking-gun poaching. Bloomberg's framing conflates *scale of talent flow* (which is real and strategically meaningful) with *pattern of theft* (which is a much narrower legal claim). Enterprises reading only the headline should discount accordingly.
